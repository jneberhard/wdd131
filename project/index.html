What is Numismatics?

The study or collection of coins, paper currency, and medals.


A Brief History of World Coinage[1]

The history of coins stretches back to the first millennium BC/BCE. Notable early examples of coins include the Lydian Lion coins, Persian daric and siglos, Tong Bei, the dirham and gold dinar. Coins are a major archaeological source of history. Coins convey information about language, administration, religion, economic conditions, and the ruler who minted those coins.

Some of the most famous and widely collected coins of antiquity are Roman coins and Greek coins. A tomb of the Chinese Shang dynasty dating back to the 11th century BCE shows what may be the first cast copper money Tong Bei. Silver and gold coins are the most common and universally recognized throughout history, even today. Mints around the world still make millions of gold and silver coins, including the Canadian Silver Maple Leaf, the American Gold Eagle, and the Australian Nugget. Copper, nickel, and other metals are also common, but in lower denominations.

History of Coins in the United States[2]

The story of U.S. coins began before the Mint's establishment in 1792, with a mix of foreign and colonial coins circulating during the Colonial Period and after the Revolutionary War. Spanish milled dollars were popular due to consistent silver content, and people cut coins into fractions for change. After the Revolutionary War, the Articles of Confederation allowed each state to produce its own coins, creating confusion with differing values across states. In 1787, Congress authorized the production of copper Fugio cents, featuring a sundial and a chain of 13 links, but the debate over national coinage continued after the Constitution was ratified in 1788.

The Coinage Act of 1792 established a national mint in Philadelphia and introduced decimal coinage based on the Spanish milled dollar. The U.S. minted copper, silver, and gold coins, including half cents, cents, half dimes, dimes, quarters, half dollars, dollars, and gold quarter eagles, half eagles, and eagles. In 1792, 1,500 silver half dimes were made but not circulated. The first circulating coins, 11,178 copper cents, were released in 1793. These cents were criticized for their large size and controversial design, leading to a quick redesign. Foreign coins were temporarily allowed to circulate until the Mint could meet demand.

The Mint struggled to produce enough coins, with copper cents being stable but insufficient due to rising copper costs. In 1857, the half cent was discontinued, and the cent was made smaller. Silver and gold coins began production in 1794 and 1795, but they did not circulate initially due to an unfavorable silver-to-gold ratio, leading to exports and melting of U.S. coins. Banks supplied precious metals and preferred large denominations, leaving smaller silver coins like half dimes, dimes, and quarters scarce. Various Acts helped improve coin production, and by 1857, Congress banned foreign coins as legal tender.

The Coinage Act of 1792 required coins to feature "LIBERTY" and the year on the obverse, and for gold and silver coins to have an eagle and "UNITED STATES OF AMERICA" on the reverse. Copper coins only had the denomination on the reverse, with a wreath becoming the standard design. Later Acts changed the inscriptions and designs we see on coins today.
