Bills

The United States currently issues seven different types of bills for circulation. These are $1, $2, $5, $10, $20, $50, and $100 notes.

U.S. paper currency is designed and updated over time. Here is a breakdown:

  1. Unique Design & Security Features: Each denomination (e.g., $1, $5, $10, etc.) has security and design elements tailored to how it is commonly used. For example, higher-value bills may have more advanced anti-counterfeiting features because they are targeted more by counterfeiters.
  2. Periodic Redesigns: The U.S. government updates Federal Reserve notes (paper money) occasionally to improve usability and security, making them harder to counterfeit while keeping them easy for the public to recognize and use.
  3. All Currency Remains Valid: No matter when a U.S. bill was issued—even as far back as 1914—it is still legal tender. This means older bills can still be used for transactions, even if newer versions exist.

For Collectors

To determine if a bill has value to collectors, here are some things to look for:

  • Misprinted bills -C ontain cut, centering, or print errors
  • Radar Notes - Palindromic serial numbers (for example 98633689)
  • Birthday Notes - Contains recognizable dates (for example 20010911)
  • Ladder Notes - Features consecutive serial numbers (for example: 23456789)
  • Repeating Numbers - Numbers repeat in pairs or triplets
  • Solid numbers - Most numbers are the same
  • Low numbers - The serial number is below 1000
  • Star Notes - The serial number ends with a star
  • Uncirculated Bills - Brand new bills, especially if another element from above is present.
